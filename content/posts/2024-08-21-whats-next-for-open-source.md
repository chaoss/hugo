## <span style="font-weight: 400;">Introduction: The Value of Working in the Open</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">The workshop opened with a compelling question: Have you ever struggled to show the value of working in the open? This question set the stage for exploring effective strategies to justify resources for open source initiatives. Georg introduced the Goal-Question-Metrics (GQM) approach, which is a recommended approach from the CHAOSS project. Because the workshop revolved around the GQM approach, we will explain it here.</span>

## <span style="font-weight: 400;">The Goal Question Metric (GQM) Approach</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">At CHAOSS, we have advocated for using the GQM because it is a structured method for deriving metrics that align with organizational goals. It involves three key steps:</span>

<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <b>Goals</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: Identify and understand your organizational goals. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">These can vary significantly but typically include objectives like recruiting talent or enhancing community engagement.</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <b>Questions</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: Break down these goals into specific, actionable questions. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">For example, to assess recruitment efforts, one might ask, &#8220;Who are important contributors?&#8221; or &#8220;How many did we help hire?&#8221;</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <b>Metrics</b><span style="font-weight: 400;">: Develop metrics to answer these questions. </span><span style="font-weight: 400;">Metrics should be operational and data-driven, such as the number of contributions by name, hiring successes, or project activity levels. Some good data points, like the number of commits, may not be relevant to the question you need to answer.</span>
</li>

## <span style="font-weight: 400;">Sharing Organizational Goals</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">In an interactive discussion, workshop participants shared diverse organizational goals, emphasizing the importance of aligning open source efforts with the broader mission statements of their organizations. We collected some bullet points on a screen and participants discussed many of them.</span>

<span style="font-weight: 400;">The most common goals included:</span>

<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <span style="font-weight: 400;">Being a thought leader: Working in the open to demonstrate the skill and knowledge of the organization to lead conversations.</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <span style="font-weight: 400;">Helping the community: Giving back to the community by working transparently and in the open.</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <span style="font-weight: 400;">Diversity: Including many voices, especially those who would not get a seat at the table if it weren’t for working in the open.</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <span style="font-weight: 400;">Tech literacy: Upskilling employees by allowing them to work in the open on new technologies.</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <span style="font-weight: 400;">Civic utility: Working in the open can bridge the gap between the government agency or organization and the constituents who are being served.</span>
</li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1">
  <span style="font-weight: 400;">Interoperability:Working in the open allows for alignment of technological advances within the organization and in the larger open source community.</span>
</li>
